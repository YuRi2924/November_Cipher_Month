CEASAR CIPHER

Named after Julius Caesar, this cipher was used by the Roman general himself to securely communicate with his commanders. Caesar shifted each letter in his messages by three positions to encode them, making it one of the earliest examples of encryption. Due to its simplicity, the Caesar Cipher is commonly used in introductory cryptography.

ATBASH CIPHER

Originating in ancient Hebrew script, the Atbash Cipher was used in early Jewish texts to encode messages. It's a simple substitution cipher where the first letter of the alphabet is replaced by the last, the second by the second-to-last, and so on. Atbash was used historically as a form of obfuscation rather than serious encryption.

VIGENERE CIPHER

A polyalphabetic substitution cipher that uses a keyword to shift letters in the plaintext, where each letter of the keyword determines the shift amount. This makes it harder to crack compared to simple shift ciphers.

ANAGRAM

An anagram is a word or phrase formed by rearranging the letters of another word or phrase, using each letter exactly once. The result often creates a new word or phrase with a different meaning, making anagrams a popular tool for wordplay and puzzles.
